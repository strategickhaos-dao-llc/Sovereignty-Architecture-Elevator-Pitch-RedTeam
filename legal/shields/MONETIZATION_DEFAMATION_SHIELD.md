## II. Defamation Defense

### Truth as Absolute Defense
All statements made in repositories, documentation, and public communications are either:
1. **Factually true** (absolute defense under Texas Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code ยง 73.001), or
2. **Protected opinion** (Milkovich v. Lorain Journal Co., 497 U.S. 1)

### Pre-Suit Notice Requirement
Under Texas Civ. Prac. & Rem. Code ยง 73.005, any defamation claim must be preceded by 30-day written notice specifying allegedly false statements and demanding correction. We commit to:
- Reviewing all notices promptly
- Correcting genuine factual errors within 10 business days
- Invoking Texas Defamation Mitigation Act protections to minimize damages

### Anti-SLAPP Protection
Under Texas Citizens Participation Act (TCPA):
- **Early Dismissal:** Frivolous defamation suits targeting free speech will be dismissed within 60 days
- **Fee Shifting:** Plaintiffs who file baseless SLAPP suits must pay our attorney fees and costs
- **Sanctions:** Courts may impose additional sanctions on vexatious litigants

### Public Figure Doctrine
Statements about public figures, corporations, government officials, or matters of public concern receive heightened First Amendment protection (New York Times Co. v. Sullivan, 376 U.S. 254). Critics must prove **actual malice** (knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard for truth).

## IV. Speech & Expression Rights

### First Amendment Protection
All content published by Strategickhaos DAO LLC is protected speech under the First Amendment:
- **Technical Documentation:** Protected as educational and scientific speech
- **Policy Advocacy:** Protected as speech on matters of public concern
- **Satire & Parody:** Protected even if offensive or critical (Hustler Magazine v. Falwell, 485 U.S. 46)
- **Online Forums:** GitHub repositories and Discord servers are modern public forums (Packingham v. North Carolina, 137 S. Ct. 1730)

### No Prior Restraint
No party may seek injunction or restraining order to prevent publication of code, documentation, or speech (Near v. Minnesota, 283 U.S. 697) except in extraordinary circumstances (national security, imminent lawless action).
